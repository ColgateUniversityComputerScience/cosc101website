Program 1: Income Taxes

In the hw3_taxes.py file, write a program that calculates how much a user owes in federal income taxes. The program should ask a user for their gross income and total deductions (in dollars) and output how much the user owes in taxes.

Your program should use the 2019 Single Tax Bracket:

Rate Taxable Income Bracket* Tax Owed
10% $0 to $9,700 10% of taxable income
12% $9,701 to $39,475 $970 plus 12% of the excess over $9,700
22% $39,476 to $84,200 $4,543 plus 22% of the excess over $39,475
24% $84,201 to $160,725 $14,382 plus 24% of the excess over $84,200
32% $160,726 to $204,100 $32,748 plus 32% of the excess over $160,725
35% $204,101 to $510,300 $46,628 plus 35% of the excess over $204,100
37% $510,301+ $153,798 plus 37% of the excess over $510,300

*Taxable income is gross income minus deductions.

The output of your program should look like this (98765 and 12200 are the inputs entered by the user):

What is your gross income? 98765
What is your total deduction? 12200
Your taxable income is $86565
You owe $14949.6 in federal taxes

This online tax calculator can help you verify your program works correctly (again, note that the above brackets are valid for the 2019 US tax year).

Problem 2: Day of the year

In the hw3_doy.py file, write a program that asks the user for a day of the year and prints the corresponding month and day of the month. If a user enters a number that is too small or too large, your program should print Enter a positive integer or There are only 365 days in a year, respectively. Your program should be designed for non-leap years.

The output of your program should be like this (46 is the input entered by the user):

Enter the day of the year: 46
Day 46 is February 15

Here is another example (400 is the input entered by the user):

Enter the day of the year: 400
There are only 365 days in a year

Your program’s output should match the above exactly. Remember to test your code to make sure it works with different inputs; don’t forget about boundary cases!

Problem 3: Adventure

In the hw3_adventure.py file, you should create a Python program that is a work of interactive fiction, a.k.a., a choose-your-own-adventure program. The starter code in hw3_adventure.py provides the start of an adventure that you may continue or replace with your own story. Your finished program needs to include at least 5 if-based control structures that affect the story or dialog (more details below). Your program should also ask for input() from the user as many times as needed.

Here are the control structures to be sure to include:

  • An if, elif, and else control structure (with exactly one elif)
  • An if, elif, elif, … and else control structure (with at least two elifs)
  • An if, else control structure (with zero elifs)
  • An if, elif, … control structure (with one or more elifs but no trailing else at all)
  • An if control structure (with no trailing elif nor trailing else at all)

Your if statements do not need to appear in the above order, and you’re welcome to add more than this, if you wish!

Please keep things light, and please be mindful of the time you spend on this problem. The goal here is not to take a lot of time.

Happy adventuring!

Testing

Please note:

  • The test program is extremely picky about spacing in the output (from print statements) your program produces. If it reports an error, it might just be the difference of a single space.

To use the test program, follow these steps:
